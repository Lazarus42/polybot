UNDERDOG ALLOCATION BACKTEST BRIEF FOR LLM REVIEW

Purpose
-------
This document summarizes the current state of an online allocation backtest for an underdog betting/market strategy. It is intended to be pasted into an LLM for further analysis, critique, and experiment design.

Important framing: this is a backtesting and allocation-design problem. Treat it as a research/engineering problem about causal online decision-making under uncertainty, fixed budgets, partial real-time market availability, noisy ROI estimates, and nonstationarity. Do not treat it as advice to wager real money.


Project Context
---------------
We have an "optimal underdog strategy" that identifies candidate opportunities across different markets. The initial simple benchmark is:

    Bet $1 on each qualifying market/opportunity.

We want to improve this by learning fixed weights from training data and then using those weights to allocate a fixed budget across future opportunities.

The complication is that in real time, the bot does not know the final set of markets that will be available over a whole week. Markets arrive/are seen chronologically, fills are limited, and the bot has to allocate causally using only information available at the time of execution.

So the central problem is:

    How should we allocate a fixed weekly budget across different markets/opportunities using fixed weights learned from historical data, when the real-time available opportunity set is unknown in advance?

We tested this in a causal online backtest.


Backtest Setup
--------------
Implemented and ran the causal online allocation backtest across:

    - 4 chronological training/holdout cuts
    - 172 policy configurations
    - 9,030 contiguous weekly-policy observations

The setup is intended to mimic how a real bot would behave:

    - Early training segment selects TP/SL parameters.
    - Later training-only calibration estimates ROI weights, thresholds, uncertainty, and weekly arrival rates.
    - Parameters are frozen before each holdout.
    - Markets are processed chronologically.
    - Allocation never sees the final weekly opportunity set.
    - Empty calendar weeks are retained.
    - Kalshi fees are applied.
    - Cent ticks and order rounding are applied.
    - Weekly budget caps are applied.
    - Liquidity caps are applied.
    - $1 orders are subject to a bot-realistic 10% triggering-fill participation limit.

Baseline Results: $1 Per Market
-------------------------------
The $1 equal-staking benchmark produced the following results:

    Training cut    Holdout             Weeks    Profit    ROI deployed    Positive weeks
    50%             Apr 2024-Sep 2025    75      $0        0%              0%
    60%             Aug 2024-Sep 2025    60      $1,370    52.0%           86.7%
    70%             Nov 2024-Sep 2025    45      $872      9.4%            80.0%
    80%             Mar 2025-Sep 2025    30      $355      2.7%            73.3%

Notes:

    - The 50% cut makes no trades because no price level has the required 30 earlier fit observations.
    - Average weekly deployment increases from $44 at the 60% cut to $432 at the 80% cut.
    - ROI falls as deployment rises.
    - The baseline remains profitable in the 60%, 70%, and 80% holdouts, but the deployed ROI decays substantially.

Preliminary interpretation:

    The edge may be real but thin.
    As deployment increases, the strategy probably starts taking lower-quality opportunities.
    A small equal-staking rule is robust, but scaling it naively may dilute the edge.


Weighted Strategy Results
-------------------------
Weighted strategies performed well in older holdouts but failed in the latest one.

Best weighted results by cut:

    - 60% cut best:
        two-stage/raw ROI, +$8,839

    - 70% cut best:
        normalized cash/profit-volume, +$7,870

    - 80% cut best weighted strategy:
        observed normalization/profit-volume, -$28

    - Every other weighted strategy lost money in the latest 30-week holdout.

Comparison with equal staking in latest holdout:

    - $1 equal staking in 80% holdout: +$355
    - Best weighted strategy in 80% holdout: -$28
    - Most weighted strategies in 80% holdout: negative

Additional market-level observation:

    - Crypto alone lost $455 in the latest $1 holdout.
    - Sports and uncategorized markets remained positive in the latest $1 holdout.

Preliminary interpretation:

    This is strong evidence that historical ROI weights are unstable across regimes.
    Raw or aggressive ROI-based allocation probably overfits older regimes.
    The issue is not merely allocation mechanics; the learned market-level ROI signal itself appears nonstationary.
    Crypto may have undergone a regime shift or may be especially noisy/unstable.


Main Conclusion
---------------
The current evidence favors equal small stakes over ROI-weighted sizing.

However, this does not necessarily mean that all weighting is bad. A better interpretation is:

    ROI-estimated weights are currently too high-variance and/or too nonstationary to be trusted as aggressive sizing multipliers.

The best production baseline right now should probably be:

    Small equal stakes, possibly with market-level risk gates.

The next research question should not be:

    "How do we maximize historical ROI using learned weights?"

It should be:

    "Can we improve equal $1 staking by removing or capping bad regimes/markets, without losing the robustness that made equal staking work?"

This means we should separate:

    1. Selection / gating:
        Which markets/opportunities are allowed?

    2. Sizing:
        How much do we stake once an opportunity is allowed?

Right now, ROI weighting is doing both. It says:

    "This market had high historical ROI, so bet much more on it."

That is dangerous if market-level ROI is unstable. Historical ROI should probably be used as a weak gating/capping signal, not as a large sizing multiplier.


Recommended Current Champion Policy
-----------------------------------
The current champion should probably be something like:

    Equal Small Stake with Market Risk Gates

Pseudo-policy:

    For each available opportunity, processed chronologically:

        1. Determine market group.
        2. Check whether market group is allowed.
        3. If allowed:
              stake $1, subject to liquidity, participation, and weekly budget caps.
           Else:
              skip.
        4. Never use the final weekly opportunity set.
        5. Never update parameters during holdout except under explicitly tested online rules.

Possible market-group allow rule:

    allow market_group if:
        - enough calibration observations exist
        - shrunk ROI or CLV is positive
        - recent rolling performance is not strongly negative
        - group-level drawdown is below threshold
        - market group is not currently in a detected bad regime

This should be tested against the plain $1 baseline.


Important Design Principle
--------------------------
Use ROI mostly as a filter/gate, not as an aggressive size multiplier.

Bad/fragile design:

    Bet much more because historical ROI was high.

Better design:

    Permit, cap, or slightly tilt exposure based on robust evidence.

In other words:

    ROI signal should answer:
        "Is this market safe/eligible enough to include?"

    Not:
        "How much should we lever this market?"


Next Experiments to Run
-----------------------

1. Equal Stake + Market Gates
-----------------------------
Test equal $1 staking with market-level filters.

Examples:

    Policy A:
        allow market m iff calibration ROI_m > 0

    Policy B:
        allow market m iff lower confidence bound ROI_m > 0

    Policy C:
        allow market m iff ROI_m > 0 in at least 3 of the last 4 rolling windows

    Policy D:
        allow market m iff recent 8/13/26-week ROI is not negative beyond threshold

    Policy E:
        allow market m iff market drawdown is below a threshold

Important: keep the stake equal once allowed. Do not size aggressively by ROI.

Goal:

    Determine whether gating can improve the $1 baseline without introducing instability.


2. Equal Stake + Crypto Ablations
---------------------------------
Because crypto lost $455 in the latest $1 holdout while sports and uncategorized markets remained positive, test:

    - $1 all markets
    - $1 excluding crypto
    - $1 crypto capped
    - $1 crypto only
    - $1 sports only
    - $1 uncategorized only
    - $1 sports + uncategorized only
    - $1 all except markets with negative recent rolling ROI

For crypto specifically, test:

    - exclude crypto entirely
    - cap crypto weekly exposure
    - cap crypto daily exposure
    - allow crypto only if recent rolling performance is positive
    - allow crypto only if CLV is positive, if CLV is available
    - allow crypto only if enough recent observations exist

Goal:

    Determine whether crypto is a persistent drag, a recent-regime drag, or just noisy.


3. Capped Tilt Weights Instead of Raw ROI Weights
------------------------------------------------
Instead of letting ROI weights create large allocation differences, test small tilts around equal stake.

Example:

    weight_m = clip(1 + alpha * z_m, lower, upper)

where:

    z_m = standardized/shrunk/uncertainty-adjusted ROI signal for market m

Test:

    alpha in {0.1, 0.25, 0.5, 1.0}
    lower/upper bounds such as:
        [0.5, 2.0]
        [0.75, 1.5]
        [0.8, 1.25]

Then stake:

    stake_i = base_stake * weight_market(i)

subject to caps.

Main question:

    Is there any stable signal in historical ROI if we only allow it to mildly tilt around equal staking?

If even mild tilts hurt, then historical ROI is probably not useful for sizing.


4. Robust-Across-Cuts Policy Selection
--------------------------------------
Do not select policies by best average profit or best single holdout.

Because 172 configurations were tested, best historical policies are likely affected by multiple testing and overfit.

For each policy p, compute:

    - profit by cut
    - ROI by cut
    - max drawdown by cut
    - worst-cut profit
    - worst-cut ROI
    - median cut profit
    - percent of cuts positive
    - latest-cut profit
    - latest-cut ROI

Rank policies using robust criteria, such as:

    Score(p) = median_profit(p) - lambda * max_drawdown(p)

or:

    Score(p) = min_cut_profit(p)

or:

    Score(p) = average_profit(p), subject to latest_cut_profit(p) >= 0

or:

    Score(p) = average_profit(p), subject to all cuts nonnegative

The current baseline should be hard to beat:

    A policy that makes less peak profit but is positive across all nonempty cuts may be better than a policy that crushes older cuts but fails in the latest cut.

Goal:

    Avoid choosing overfit policies that look good only because of policy search over many configurations.


5. Rolling / Recency-Aware Weights
----------------------------------
All-history ROI appears stale. Test recency-sensitive signals.

Possible windows:

    - last 4 weeks
    - last 8 weeks
    - last 13 weeks
    - last 26 weeks
    - last 52 weeks

Possible exponential decay:

    ROI_m,t = sum_{s < t} gamma^(t-s) * profit_m,s
              /
              sum_{s < t} gamma^(t-s) * deployed_m,s

Test:

    gamma in {0.90, 0.95, 0.98, 0.995}

But again, use recency-weighted ROI mainly for:

    - gating
    - caps
    - mild tilts

not aggressive allocation.

Goal:

    Determine whether market edges are regime-dependent and whether recent performance is more predictive than all-history performance.


6. Delta vs $1 Baseline Diagnostics
-----------------------------------
For every policy p and every week t, compute:

    delta_profit_{p,t} = weekly_profit_{p,t} - weekly_profit_$1,t

Then summarize by cut and policy family:

    - mean delta
    - median delta
    - percent of weeks better than $1
    - worst week versus $1
    - best week versus $1
    - cumulative delta
    - max drawdown of cumulative delta
    - skewness / tail behavior if useful

Table:

    Cut | Policy family | Mean delta | Median delta | % weeks better | Worst delta week | Cumulative delta

This answers:

    Are weighted policies systematically adding value,
    or are they occasionally levering into lucky weeks while worsening the left tail?

Hypothesis:

    Weighted policies have fat positive tails in older holdouts but worse downside / left-tail behavior in the latest holdout.


7. Policy Family Median, Not Just Best Policy
---------------------------------------------
For each policy family, report:

    - best policy
    - median policy
    - 25th percentile
    - 75th percentile
    - worst policy
    - percent profitable
    - percent beating $1
    - latest holdout median performance

Policy families might include:

    - equal stake
    - observed normalization

    - two-stage raw ROI
    - two-stage profit-volume
    - availability-adjusted
    - rank-greedy
    - Kelly/fractional edge sizing, if implemented

Goal:

    Avoid being fooled by the best member of a large, overfit family.


8. Selection vs Sizing Decomposition
------------------------------------
Decompose the weighted policies into:

    - Which opportunities they selected/skipped.
    - How much they sized each selected opportunity.

For each weighted policy, compare against $1 baseline:

    A. Same selected opportunities as weighted policy, but equal $1 stakes.
    B. Same selected opportunities as $1 baseline, but weighted sizing.
    C. Full weighted policy.

This tells us whether the policy's value/damage comes from:

    - better/worse selection
    - better/worse sizing
    - both

Hypothesis:

    The weighted policies may be selecting okay opportunities but sizing badly due to unstable ROI weights.


9. Market-Level Stability Analysis
----------------------------------
For each market group, compute:

    - profit by cut
    - ROI by cut
    - deployed amount by cut
    - number of trades by cut
    - positive-week rate by cut
    - rolling ROI
    - drawdown
    - CLV if available
    - correlation between training ROI and holdout ROI
    - correlation between recent ROI and next-period ROI

Especially inspect:

    - crypto
    - sports
    - uncategorized

Key question:

    Does market-level ROI have any out-of-sample persistence?

If the correlation between training ROI and holdout ROI is near zero or negative, do not use historical ROI for sizing.


10. Budget Utilization / Capacity Curve
---------------------------------------
The deployed ROI falls as weekly deployment rises:

    - 60% cut: average weekly deployment around $44, ROI 52.0%
    - 80% cut: average weekly deployment around $432, ROI 2.7%

This suggests capacity constraints or edge dilution.

Run capacity curves:

    weekly_budget in:
        25, 50, 100, 250, 500, 1000, 2000

    participation in:
        0.01, 0.025, 0.05, 0.10

For each, evaluate:

    - total profit
    - ROI
    - average deployed
    - percent budget used
    - max drawdown
    - positive weeks
    - profit per week

Goal:

    Estimate how much capital the strategy can absorb before the edge decays.


11. Regime Shift / Time Trend Analysis
--------------------------------------
Because the latest holdout is much worse for weighted strategies and ROI falls over time, test for regime shifts.

Diagnostics:

    - rolling weekly ROI for $1 strategy
    - rolling weekly ROI by market
    - rolling number of opportunities
    - rolling average odds/price level
    - rolling fill rate
    - rolling market mix
    - rolling profit decomposition by market
    - compare pre-2025 vs 2025
    - compare latest 30 weeks vs earlier holdouts

Possible explanations:

    - the underdog edge is decaying
    - market composition changed
    - crypto market behavior changed
    - fills became worse
    - the strategy scaled into weaker opportunities
    - TP/SL parameters selected on old data no longer fit
    - ROI weights overfit market groups that stopped working

Goal:

    Determine whether the edge is structurally decaying or just unstable across market categories.


12. CLV-Based Diagnostics, If Available
---------------------------------------
If closing line value is available, use it heavily.

For each policy and market group, compute:

    - average CLV
    - CLV by cut
    - CLV by market
    - correlation between CLV and realized profit
    - CLV for weighted strategies vs $1
    - CLV for crypto vs sports/uncategorized

If a strategy has positive realized ROI but no CLV, it may be lucky.
If a strategy has bad realized ROI but positive CLV, it may still have a real edge but high variance.

Potential robust gate:

    allow market iff recent CLV is positive or lower-confidence-bound CLV is positive.

Goal:

    Find a less noisy proxy for true edge than realized ROI.


13. Threshold and Price-Level Robustness
----------------------------------------
The 50% cut makes no trades because no price level has the required 30 earlier fit observations.

Test sensitivity to:

    - minimum fit observations:
        10, 20, 30, 50, 100

    - price level grouping:
        exact cent level
        bucketed levels
        wider price buckets
        market-type + price bucket
        global fallback when market-specific data is sparse

    - shrinkage:
        price-level ROI shrunk to market ROI
        market ROI shrunk to global ROI
        hierarchical shrinkage

Goal:

    Avoid brittle sparsity rules that create no-trade behavior or overfit small cells.


14. Hierarchical Shrinkage for ROI Estimates
--------------------------------------------
Raw ROI by market/price level is noisy. Use shrinkage.

Example:

    shrunk_roi_m = alpha_m * observed_roi_m + (1 - alpha_m) * global_roi

where:

    alpha_m = n_m / (n_m + k)

Test:

    k in {10, 25, 50, 100, 250}

For price level cells:

    shrunk_roi_cell =
        alpha_cell * observed_roi_cell
        + (1 - alpha_cell) * parent_market_roi

Then parent market ROI itself is shrunk to global ROI.

Use lower-confidence-bound ROI:

    lcb_roi = shrunk_roi - z * standard_error

Test:

    z in {0.0, 0.5, 1.0, 1.64, 1.96}

Use LCB for gating, not aggressive sizing.

Goal:

    Reduce overreaction to noisy historical winners.


15. Drawdown and Tail Risk
--------------------------
For each policy, calculate:

    - maximum drawdown
    - longest losing streak
    - worst week
    - worst 4-week period
    - worst 8-week period
    - CVaR / average of worst x% weeks
    - standard deviation of weekly profit
    - downside deviation
    - profit skew

A policy with higher average profit but much worse drawdown may be worse than $1 equal staking.

Goal:

    Quantify whether weighted allocation is adding unacceptable tail risk.


Suggested Final Decision Criteria
---------------------------------
A new allocation policy should not replace $1 equal staking unless it satisfies most of:

    - positive in the latest 80% holdout
    - positive or at least not meaningfully negative across all nonempty cuts
    - beats $1 on cumulative profit
    - does not materially worsen max drawdown
    - does not rely on one market group, especially crypto
    - has reasonable budget utilization
    - has policy-family-level robustness, not just one lucky configuration
    - passes delta-vs-$1 diagnostics
    - remains positive under slightly different cuts, e.g. 65%, 75%, 85%
    - remains positive under lower participation assumptions, e.g. 5%
    - has interpretable failure modes

Potential production choice right now:

    Champion:
        $1 equal staking with current bot-realistic constraints.

    Challenger 1:
        $1 equal staking excluding or capping crypto.

    Challenger 2:
        $1 equal staking with market gates based on recent/shrunk/LCB ROI.

    Challenger 3:
        capped mild-tilt weights around equal stake.

Avoid for now:

    - raw ROI weighting
    - aggressive profit-volume weighting
    - unconstrained normalized weighting
    - strategies selected only because they were best among 172 configurations
    - policies that lose in the latest holdout
